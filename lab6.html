A Summarized History of Computers

The First Digital Computers

The first digital computer was called the ENIAC, which stands for Electronic Numerical Integrator And Calculator. It was created at the
University of Pennsylvania for military research funding and its main purpose was to quickly calculate trajectories for artillery shells.
It could only run one program at a time, which had to be fed into the computer as a sequence punch cards.

In the 1960s, the not so mini Minicomputers were created. They were about the size of a refrigerator, and affordable enough for most
universities. They had multiple terminals, which consist of a keyboard and monitor, but didn't have their own processors. The beneficial
thing about Minicomputers was that different people could run programs on the same computer at the same time. If multiple programs were
running, the minicomputer would share the processor cycles among the different programs.

The First Desktop Computers

The first desktop computers became available during the late 1970s and early 1980s. The first one, called the Apple II, was created in
1977. Apple computers, which later went on to be called Mac, was the first computer to feature a Floppy Disk Drive, Mouse, and Windows
Graphical User Interface.

The second desktop computer called IBM PC was created in 1981. It used Microsoft's DOS Operating System. Microsoft eventually patents
the term Windows, and in 1985 Windows 1.1 was released.


   Image of Apple II

Computers Today

Computers today have become so advanced that they are small enough to fit in our pockets through phones, and on our wrists as watches
and fitbits. They are even embedded in our everyday appliances like TVs, refrigerators, kitchen tools, and cars. Each computer, in
all of its forms, contains its own processor. This shows how far we have come because even the much bigger Minicomputers didn't have
their own processors.

The leading creators of computers today are Apple, Google, and Microsoft, each of which specialize in either
or some of desktop, tablet-like, and mobile computing.
